Rudin, Chapter I

The Real and Complex Number Systems

This section was, by far, the easiest section to understand in Rudin. Most of the things feel intuitive and straightforward, with the only big challenge being the construction of $\mathbb R$ from $\mathbb Q$ via Dedekind cuts.

Proof

Since $x < y$, then $y - x > 0$. By the Archimedean property, there exists a positive integer $n$ such that \[n(y - x) > 1.\] We may use the Archimedean property again to obtain integers $s$ and $t$ such that $s < nx$ and $t < -nx$. It follows that \[-t < nx < s.\] Consider the set of integers between $-t$ and $s$. This is a finite subset of $\mathbb Z$, hence we may consider some $m$ such that $-t \leq m \leq s$, and \[m - 1 \leq nx < m.\] From the first inequality, we see that $nx + 1 < ny$. From the third inequality, adding $1$ to everything implies that $m \leq nx + 1$. This results in \[nx < m \leq nx + 1 < ny.\] Since $n$ is positive, we may divide by $n$ to obtain \[x < \frac{m}{n} < y.\] Since $m, n \in \mathbb Z$, then $m/n \in \mathbb Q$, and we have found a rational number between $x$ and $y$.
