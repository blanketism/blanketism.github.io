Market Basics

Financial derivatives

A beginner-friendly guide to trading basics: how to open an account, manage risk, size positions, and avoid the mistakes that usually come before real progress.

What trading is

Trading is the act of taking positions in markets with a plan for why you expect price to move, where you will exit if you are wrong, and how much you are willing to lose if the idea fails. Good trading is less about prediction and more about risk control.

Getting started with a brokerage

Most beginners start by opening a brokerage account, which is the platform that lets you buy and sell stocks, ETFs, options, and other products. The signup process usually looks like this:

  • Create an account and verify your identity.
  • Link a bank account and move in funds.
  • Choose the account type you actually need, such as a cash account or margin account.
  • Review the platform's fee schedule, order types, and settlement rules before placing anything.

Order basics

The first practical things to understand are market orders, limit orders, and stop orders. Market orders prioritize execution, limit orders prioritize price, and stop orders are usually used as triggers for exits or entries. Beginners should know exactly which one they are placing before clicking submit.

Risk management

Risk management is the core of surviving long enough to get better. A common rule is to risk only a small fraction of your account on any single trade, often around 1% or less. That means a losing trade should be small enough that it does not change your behavior or force you to break your plan.

A simple template is: decide your entry, stop loss, and target before the trade starts. If the trade does not make sense with those three numbers, do not take it.

Position sizing

Position sizing answers a simple question: how many shares or contracts should you buy? The answer comes from your account size and the distance to your stop loss.

If you are risking $R$ dollars and your stop is $d$ dollars away, then a rough position size is $R / d$. For example, if you are willing to lose $50$ and your stop is $2$ away, the position size is about $25$ shares. The exact sizing changes when you include contracts, commissions, and slippage.

What to avoid early on

  • Trading too large because the setup feels "certain".
  • Adding to losers without a plan.
  • Using leverage before understanding how fast losses can compound.
  • Confusing a good idea with a good risk/reward setup.
  • Skipping a journal, which makes it hard to learn from mistakes.

A simple starter checklist

  • Open a brokerage account and understand the fee structure.
  • Choose a market and one setup to study first.
  • Define your maximum risk per trade and per day.
  • Write down the entry, stop, and target before entering.
  • Review every trade afterward to see whether you followed the plan.

Why it matters

Trading is not just about finding opportunities. It is about staying consistent when the market is noisy, protecting capital when you are wrong, and avoiding the temptation to improvise. That discipline matters more than any single trade.
